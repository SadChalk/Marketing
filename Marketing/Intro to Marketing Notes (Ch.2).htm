2-1 The External Marketing Environment

PESTEL Model

  • Legal
CREST Model
  • Competitive
  • Regulatory
  • Economic

Marketers must keep an eye on the pulse of their target market. With the connected digital world they can do this through environmental scanning.

To understand the external environment, one must consider each of the components that make up the CREST model: competitive, regulatory, economic, social, and technological forces that impact the company but also impact each other (e.g. plant based proteins).

Most important to remember is that most impacts are a result of social forces.

General Ideas
  • The Marketing mix is designed to appeal to a specific group of potential buyers -- the target market.
  • Target market -- a group of potential customers for whom a company's product offers the greatest value.
  • Marketing mix must be adjusted to reflect the changing needs of the target market.
  • As there are many other external factors that the company has no control over, which will affect the target market.

2-2 Competitive Factors

General Ideas

Competitive environment: competitors to the business, both direct and indirect, must be considered as well as their size and number.

Marketers must constantly plan as they deal with these five forces of competition:

  • Substitute -- satisfy the same need
  • New Entrants -- enter when barriers to entry are removed
  • Suppliers -- business partners who want to charge the highest price for materials
  • Buyers -- business partners who want to pay the least to maximize their profit

2-3 Regulatory Factors

General Ideas
  • Understanding the laws and restrictions is critical, but so is tracking and predicting them.
  • Sometimes, sensing trends and being proactive can limit the impact of government regulation.
Relevant Examples:
Competition Act Promotes the efficiency and adaptability of the Canadian economy
Expands opportunities for Canadian participation in world markets whilst at the same time recognizing the role of foreign competition in Canada. Ensures that small and medium sized enterprises have an opportunity to participate in the Canadian economy. Provides consumers with competitive prices and product choices.
Consumer Packaging and Labelling Act Requires prepackaged consumer products to bear meaningful labelling information to help consumers make informed purchasing decisions. It prohibits false or misleading representations and sets out specifications for mandatory label information.
Trade-marks Act Regulates and protects trade names and trademarks
Textile labelling act Requires that textile articles bear accurate and meaningful labelling information to help consumers make informed purchasing decisions. It prohibits false or misleading representations and sets out specifications for mandatory label information
Health Canada's Food and Drugs Act \& Regulations Establishes standards for the safety and nutritional quality of all foods sold in canada
Motor Vehicle Safety Act Regulates the safety standards for the manufacture and importation of motor vehicles
Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act Supports and promotes electronic commerce by protecting personal information tha tis collected, used, or disclosed in certain circumstances, by providing for te use of electronic means to communicate or record information or transactions
Privacy Act Governs the personal information handling practices of federal government institutions. Applies to all the personal information the federal government collects, uses, and discloses--whether about individuals or federal employees.

Legislation can be Federal, Provincial, Municipal, or Self-Imposed (ASC, CMA)

2-4 Economic Factors

Consumer Income
  • Education is the primary determinant for earning potential.
  • Income is a key demographic variable marketers use to make target-market decisions.
  • Geography can also reflect income.
  • However, our digital footprint can make this a confusing variable for marketers to use when trying to reach consumers via social media.
  • Average HHLD (Household) debt in Canada reached $2.6 trillion, the highest rate of debt relative to any of the G7 countries.
Purchasing Power
  • Purchasing Power = Income - Cost of living
  • Discretionary Income = Income > Cost of living
  • The higher the discretionary income, the higher the purchasing power
  • In Canada the cost of living has soared in Vancouver and Toronto due to housing prices
Inflation
  • Inflation impacts consumers' purchasing power if salary increases don't keep up with inflation.
  • Inflation pressures consumers to make more economical purchases to maintain their standard of living.
  • Marketers must develop strategies that acknowledge the impact of inflation but still compel consumers to buy,
Recession
  • Period of economic activity categorized by negative growth
  • Price value relationship becomes increasingly important when making purchases
  • Can be provincial
  • COVID likely resulted in what will be a prolonged recession.

2-6 Demographics

  • Strongly related to behavior - Age, Ethnicity, and Location
Generation Z, b. 1995 - 2009 - Attitudes
- Access to information
- Brand consciousness
- Technical sophistication
- Purchasing Power to Match (Low cost of living due to most still being covered by parents)
Generation Y (Millennials), b. 1979 - 2000 - 9 million in Canada
- Heightened sense of social responsibility
- Trophy kids: high expectations in workplace with increased sense of entitlement
- Work-Life balance
- Struggling within career
- Entrepreneurial
Generation X, b. 1966 - 1978 - 7 million in Canada
- Savvy and cynical consumers
- Spending is reflective of the many demands on them
- Time poor, service dependent
- Career advancement impacted by the previous generation not retiring
- Further impacted by the many unknowns due to COVID-19 pandemic
Baby Boomers, b. 1947 - 1965 - Living longer, working longer, remaining active after retirement
- Powerful demographic that could indeed support the non-working population--repeat of the 1950's
- Oldest are approaching 80 years
- Spending power of $1 trillion.
- Largest marketing demographic, ever.
Other Factors to consider - Population Shifts
- 50 percent of the population lives in four major regions
- immigration = Most of the population growth
- immigrants settle within urban markets = cultural diversity
- Ethnic and cultural diversity
- More than 200 languages spoken in Canada
- More than 20% of visible minorities in Canada under 15 years old--longer term impact is powerful
- This year (2022) expected another 500 000 immigrants
- Highest growing population rate of any G7 nation at 1.4%

2-7 Technological Factors

General Ideas
  • Some tech companies are creating technology to such an extent that they must be considered a "force within the force" of tech.

2-8 Corporate Social Responsibility and Ethical Behaviour in Business

Corporate Social Responsibility
Social Needs -> Fuelled Competition to serve needs -> Driving Tech Innovation -> Creating Economic Peaks and Valleys -> Requiring Regulations
  • Economic Responsibility -- Benefit the economy
  • Legal Responsibility -- Obey the law
  • Ethical Responsibility -- Obey the Moral law
  • Philanthropic Responsibility -- Giving back to society
Ethical Behaviour
  • Ethics: standard of behaviour by which conduct is judged
  • Laws are the values and standards enforced by courts
  • Ethics are moral principles and values
  • Morals: rules developed as a result of cultural norms and values
Ethical Development
  • Preconventional morality -- childlike, what will be immediately punished; absolute right and wrong
  • Conventional morality -- society's expectations - from a marketer's perspective - how it would be viewed by other or would there be legal action.
  • Postconventional morality -- how we see ourselves in the long run.
Ethical Decision Making

Factors Influencing Ethical Decision Making

  • Extent of all ethical problems within the organization
  • Top-management actions on ethics
  • Potential magnitude of the consequences
  • Social Consensus
  • Probability of harmful outcome
  • Length of time between decision and onset of consequences
  • Number of people affected
Ethical Guidelines
Code of Ethics
  • A guideline to help marketing managers and other make better decisions
Advantage
  • Helps employees identify the business practices their firm recognizes as being acceptable
  • Can be an effective internal control on behaviour
  • More desirable than external controls such as government regulation
  • Helps employees avoid confusion when determining whether their decisions are ethical
  • Process of formulating code of ethics facilitate discussion amongst employees
