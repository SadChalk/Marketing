Intro to Marketing Notes (Ch.5)

5-1 The Importance of Understanding Consumer Behaviour

General Ideas
  • Consumer behaviour describes how consumers make purchase decisions and how they use and dispose of the purchased goods or services.
  • Without an understanding, the development of an effective marketing mix is highly compromised
  • Consumer preferences are continually changing.
  • Study how consumers make purchase decisions
  • How they use the purchase
  • How they dispose
  • What factors influence the purchase, use, and disposal
Consumer decision-making process
  1. Need recognition
  2. Information Search
  3. Evaluation of Alternatives
  4. Purchase
  5. Post-purchase Behaviour
  • Not always in this order
  • Not all steps included
  • Technology has impacted this as well.

5-2 Components of the Consumer decision-making process

Step 1: Need Recognition
  • Need recognition -- result of an imbalance between actual and desired state
  • Triggered by exposure to an external stimulus
  • Stimulus: any unit of input affecting one or more of the 5 senses
  • Internal stimuli: Occurrences you experience
  • External stimuli: Influence from an outside source
  • Need is a state of being where we desire something we do not possess
  • Want is for a particular product or service that will satisfy a need
  • Marketers create wants on the part of the customer
Step 2: Information Search

  • Internal information search
  • Memory search

The more a consumer knows and feels comfortable about a decision the less-likely they will search

Information Search

Factors affecting external search could include:

  • Perceived risk
  • Knowledge
  • Prior experience
  • Level of interest in the good or service
  • Confidence in decision-making ability

Evoked set or Consideration set

  • Most preferred alternatives
  • Don't consider all brands
  • But seriously consider a smaller set
  • Too many choices can cause confusion or delay them or cause them not to buy at all
Steps 3 and 4: Evaluation of Alternatives and Purchase

Consideration Set

  • Will develop a list of criteria to compare the alternatives
  • Product attributes(s)
  • Cutoffs - minimum and maximum levels of an attribute
  • Rank the attributes with the different alternatives
  • Other criteria
  • Goal of Marketing Manager (you, the reader) is to figure out which attitude is most important to the customer
  • Decides to buy OR not to buy at all
Step 5: Post-purchase Behaviour
Provide decision confirmation from evaluation of alternatives through to purchase to post-purchase, or may result in cognitive dissonance (buyer's remorse)
Cognitive dissonance is defined a inner tension that a consumer experiences after recognizing an inconsistency between behaviour, values, or opinions
Marketers -- important to reduce any lingering doubts that the decision was sound
Marketers provided decision confirmation support -- at beginning of evaluation stage of alternative stage, through the purchase and into post-purchase
Post purchase communication by sellers and dissonance-reducing statements in instruction booklets may help customer to feel good about their purchase

#####

Factors Determining Involvement Levels

Previous experience

  • Level of involvement will decrease with more experience
  • Familiar with product and know they will be satisfied

Interest

  • Higher interest categories, like cars, music, movies will have more involvement

Perceived risk of negative consequences

  • The riskier/more negative the purchase could become the more involved the consumer is in the purchase; this also applies to financial risk

Situation

Psychological or Financial risk

  • If consumers feel they are making the wrong decision and it is causing them anxiety

Social Visibility

  • When social opinions of the individual could be affected
  • Or social visibility of the product is high (e.g., jewellery)
Marketing Implications of Involvement
  • Marketing strategy varies according to level of involvement
  • Low involvement: Strategy may focus on in-store (packaging, in-store promotions)
  • Extensive involvement: Strategy may focus on what has been learned from the marketing environment

Cultural and Social Factors affect customer buying decisions

  • Cultural influences on consumer buying decisions
  • Broadest and deepest influence
  • Pervasive - includes all things consumers do without conscious choice because their culture's values, customs, rituals are ingrained in their daily habits
  • Functional - human interaction creates values and prescribes acceptable behaviour for each culture
  • Learned - you learn it--not born with it
  • Dynamic - adapts to changing needs of environment
  • The most defining element: values
  • Correspond to consumption patterns
  • Give rise to beliefs, which lead to attitudes
  • Cultural influences on consumer buying decisions
  • Subculture
  • Homogenous group of people who share elements of the overall culture and also have their own unique cultural elements
  • Attitudes, values, and purchase decisions are similar
  • Social Class
  • Group nearly equal in status or esteem
  • Combination of income, education, wealth
  • Education is the most reliable factor

5-4 Social Influences on Consumer Buying Decisions

Reference groups

All the formal and informal groups that influence purchase behaviour

Direct F2F membership

  • Touch people's lives directly
  • Primary:
  • Small, informal groups
  • Family, friends, co-workers
  • Secondary:
  • Large, formal
  • Less consistent interaction
  • Clubs, professional associations, religious affiliation

Indirect non-membership

  • Aspirational reference groups
  • Person would like to join
  • Person must confirm to this group's norms, values, attitudes
  • Ex. Athletes
  • Non- aspirational / dissociate groups
  • We try to maintain a distance from these groups

For Marketers, reference groups have 3 important implications

  • They serve as information sources and influence perceptions
  • They affect individuals aspiration levels
  • Their norms either constrain or stimulate consumer behaviour

Marketers are always looking for trendsetters

 ex. Snowboard Manufacturer
  • Look for trendsetters and how they personalize the way they use their equipment
  • As it becomes more popular will lose appeal of trendsetter and they look for new ways to customize

Opinion Leaders/Influencers

Reference groups who influence others

  • First to try new products
  • Self-indulgent
  • Vary by category
  • Ex. Teenagers, because of their willingness to experiment are key opinion leaders for new technologies like texting
  • Now easier to find with social media
  • 20% of online fashion purchases were influenced by social median and more than 40% made a purchase that an influencer promoted

Family

  • Most important social institution for many consumers, strongly influencing their values
  • Socialization process
  • Influences values, attitudes, and self-concept
  • Variety of roles
  • Initiator (plant the seed), influencer (those in the family whose opinion is valued), decisionmaker (the one who makes the decision to buy), purchaser (spends the money), consumer (user of product)

5-5 Individual Influences on Consumer Buying Decisions

Gender

  • Psychological differences between men and women result in different needs
  • Men and women go through the same decision-making process
  • Women go through the process more than once, as they want to make the perfect decision
  • Trends of changing roles of men and women mean marketers must develop new strategies
  • In 60% of Canadian households, a male is either primary grocery shopper or participates in grocery shopping
  • By 2015, stay at home dads have risen by 12%

Age and family life cycle

  • How old a consumer is will indicate the products they are interested in buying
  • Gen Z shop at retail, Millennials shop more online
  • Marketers define their target markets in terms of family life
  • Also, non-traditional life cycle paths should be considered: divorcees, life long singles, and childless couples.

Personality

  • How people react to situations
  • Combines psychological makeup and environmental forces
  • It also includes their dominant characteristics
  • Marketers believe this influences the brands people purchase

Self Concept

  • How consumers perceive themselves
  • While it can change, the change is gradual
  • Through self concept, people define their identity
  • Self concept combines ideal self image and real self image
  • Important component of self concept is body image
  • Many marketers are developing products that are aimed at aging baby boomers who are concerned about their age and appearance

Lifestyle

  • Both personality and self concept are reflected in lifestyle
  • Lifestyle is a way of living, identified by a person's activities, interests, and opinions.
  • Psychographics is the technique used to analyze consumer lifestyles
  • This can be used in segmenting and targeting consumers

5-6 Psychological Influences on Consumer Buying Behaviour

Perception

  • How we select, organize, and interpret stimuli into a meaningful coherent picture
  • How we see the world around us and we recognize we need help in making a purchase decision
  • We cannot respond to everything, so we have selective exposure
  • Ex. Shape of packaging of a product, colour to signify a product is healthy, etc.
  • Selective distortion consumers change or distort information that conflicts with their feelings or beliefs
  • Selective retention consumers remember only information that supports their benefits
  • Threshold of perception: the minimum difference a customer will notice

Motivation

  • Major forces influencing consumer to buy:

  • Self-actualization needs,

  • Esteem needs,
  • Social needs,
  • Safety needs,
  • Physiological needs

Learning

  • Process that creates changes in behaviour, immediate, or expected through experience and practice
  • Two types of learning: Experimental, experience changes your behaviour; Conceptual, not acquired through direct experience
  • Reinforcement and repetition boost learning
  • Repetition is key strategy for learning

Beliefs and attitudes

  • Organized pattern of knowledge that an individual holds as true
  • Beliefs can be based on knowledge, hearsay, or faith
  • Attitude: learned tendency to respond consistently towards a given object
  • Changing beliefs may need to happen if a good or service is not meeting its goals. This can happen in three ways: Changing beliefs about the brand's attitudes, Changing importance of beliefs, and Adding new beliefs.
Consumer behaviour elements -- working together
  • Effective marketers carefully study their target markets all the time to see the changes in their buying decisions
  • Adjustments are made to the marketing mix to meet the changing consumer needs and help to move the consumer through the buying process.
