Intro to Marketing Notes (Ch.10)

10-1 The Importance of Products

New Products
  • Sustains growth
  • Increases revenue and profits
  • Replaces obsolete items

  • Meets ever-changing new consumer wants

    • New technologies
    • Shrinking product life cycle
  • Most innovative in 2019: Google (Parent Company: Alphabet)
  • Some truly Canadian innovations include: basketball, insulin (though it wasn't meant to be a product when first invented), IMAX, electric wheelchair, Java programming peanut butter

  • To be successful

    • Brand incrementality
    • Mass potential
    • Category distinction
    • Longevity
    • Specific target appeal
Categories of New Products

See: Exhibit 10.1 New Product Categories

  • New to the World

    • Discontinuous innovation
    • Creation of entirely new market
    • Smallest category

  • New Product Lines

    • Not previously offered by the firm
    • Allows a firm to enter an established market
    • Ex. Tim Horton's new soup, chilies

  • Additions to Product Lines

    • New products to supplement existing product lines
    • Capitalize on brand equity
    • Ex. New style of jacket from Zara

  • Improvements or Revisions to Existing Products

    • New and improved
    • Significantly or slightly changed
    • Could be a package improvement
    • Ex. Gillette Fusion 5 Proshield

  • Repositioned Products

    • Existing products targeted at new markets or new segments
    • Ex. Sylvania, Find Your Tru Light - Tru wave technology to mimic natural light

  • Lower-Priced Products

    • Provide performance similar to competing brands at a lower price
    • Ex. Ring doorbell - security system

10-2 Product Development Steps

The New-Product Development Process

Key to Success for "innovation" -- according to Booz Allen Hamilton

  • Long-term commitment
  • Company-specific approach driven by corporate objectives and strategies
  • Capitalize on experience -- to achieve and maintain a competitive advantage
  • Establish the environment -- management style, top support and all the way through the company

Need a new product strategy which is in line with company strategy

  • General guidelines for generating, screening, and evaluating new product ideas

See: Exhibit 10.2 New-Product Development Process

  1. Idea Generation

New Ideas

  • Customers -- based on their wants and needs -- ex. DeWalt Insights Forum
  • Employees -- all employees -- Ex. Fashion companies: Prada, Kering, have a hackathon to bring people together to generate ideas
  • Distributors -- ask what needs are not being met -- Ex. Rubbermaid sidekick lunch box
  • Competitors -- market intelligence; copy or make a product better
  • Vendors -- partnerships -- Ex. Jones Soda Big Gulps in 7/11
  • R \& D -- Canada trying to become a hub for innovation; generates 4% of the world knowledge but is only 0.5% of population; Ex. Corning glass for iPhone screens.
  • Consultants
Creativity
  • Brainstorming -- think of unlimited ways to vary a product or solve a problem; no idea is a bad idea
  • Focus-group exercises
  • How to Enhance Creativity
2. Idea Screening
  • The elimination of ideas that are inconsistent with the organization's new-product strategy

  • Good screening requires:

    • that management have a clear understanding of what their business is all about
    • an understanding of business objectives
    • a degree of tolerance for risk and uncertainty
  • Concept tests: Survey consumer reactions to description; react to a new product idea; most important score = DWB = Definity would buy score
3. Business Analysis

  • Second stage of screening, which considers:

    • Demand - overall economic trend
    • costs
    • sales
    • profitability
4. Development
  • Prototype

  • Marketing strategy

    • Packaging, branding, labelling
    • Preliminary promotion, price and distribution
  • Manufacturing--feasibility of doing so cost effectively relative to price

  • Laboratory tests can occur

    • User safety
  • In-home tests
5. Test Marketing
  • Limited introduction of a product with an associated marketing program to determine a reaction - a necessary step
  • London a great city for test markets

  • High costs -- takes one year longer; costs more money

    • Financial
    • Exposure to competition

  • Alternatives

    • Simulated laboratory tests
    • Computer simulation
    • Internet
6. Commercialization

  • The decision to market the product

    • All elements involved in getting the product to market begin with the decision to go.
    • Time--varies depending on the company and complexity
    • Ex. Gillette Mach3 razor - $750M in development + $300M USD for first year marketing budget
Keys to Success
  • History of listening
  • Vision of what the future holds
  • Strong leadership
  • Commitment to the new-product development
  • Project-based approach
  • Perfection at each stage
Failures
  • Incongruity -- product and market
  • Takes too long
  • Lack of resources
  • Poor initial product reviews and few changes made
  • Poor execution of the marketing mix

10-3 Global Issues

Increasing Globalization
  • In many multinational corporations, every product is developed for potential worldwide distribution, and unique market requirements are built in whenever possible.
  • Some global marketers design their products to meet regulations in their major markets and then, if necessary, meet smaller market's requirements country by country, Ex. Domino's -- specific toppings based on where pizza is being sold

10-5 Product Life Cycles

See: Exhibits 10.4, 10.5

Introduction
  • High failure rate
  • Little competition
  • Frequent product modifications
  • Limited distribution

  • Costs

    • Marketing and production--high

  • Sales

    • Increase slowly

  • Promotion

    • Focuses on product awareness and information--primary demand
Growth

  • Sales

    • Grow at increasing rate

  • Competition

    • Intensifies

  • Profits

    • Rise rapidly, peak, fall off

  • Aggressive brand advertising

    • Communication of differences
  • Distribution is key
Maturity
  • Sales increase at decreasing rate
  • Market saturation

  • Product

    • Lines lengthened, service important, product design
  • Pricing competition

  • Distribution

    • Shelf space

  • Promotion

    • Heavy consumer promotion
  • Niche
Decline

  • Sales

    • Decline--length varies
  • Requires a deliberate strategy
Implications for Marketing Management
  • PLC encourages marketers to plan vs. react
  • Useful for predicting or forecasting
