Intro to Marketing Notes (Ch.9)

9-1 Products

Definition

Anything both favourable and unfavourable received by a person in an exchange for possession, consumption, attention, or short-term use

Note: not all products are owned (a car, candy, etc.); some are intangible (services, ideas, etc.).

9-2 Types of consumer products

List
  • Convenience goods
  • Shopping goods
  • Specialty goods
  • Unsought goods
Convenience Products
  • Inexpensive
  • Takes little effort to shop for
  • Unwilling to shop extensively for it
  • Requires wide distribution
  • Impulse goods
  • Requires little involvement (information search) -- you already know you want it.

Ex: Fast-Food

Shopping Products
  • Require comparison shopping
  • Customers willing to put the time and effort into shopping
  • Unlike convenience products, people are willing to put more effort into comparing price, quality, etc.

Ex: Groceries

Types of consumer products:

#####

Homogenous Heterogenous
- Product perceived as similar to something else
- Look for the lowest-priced brand that has desired features		 - Product is perceived as different in terms of features, quality, and performance
- Consumers shop around but have a hard time comparing to other products because of variation
Specialty Products
  • Consumers search for these products extensively
  • Unwilling to accept substitutes
  • Unique characteristics
  • Use selective advertising
  • Distribution is limited to one or very few outlets
  • Brand names and quality of service are important
  • Once people have their mind made up about the product, they are unlikely to change it.

Ex: Cars

Unsought Products
  • Unknown to the consumer
  • Known product but not actively sought after
  • New products -- until consumer is aware of them
  • Things like burial plots are unlikely to be thought about until they are needed -- this means that these products need more aggressive adverising.

Ex: Insurance

9-3 Product Items, Lines, and Mixes

Product Item
  • Distinct offering

Ex: Campbell's Tomato Soup

Product line
  • Group of closely related product items

Ex: Campbell's Condensed Soups

Product Mix
  • All of the products a company sells

Ex: Campbell's Soups, Sauces, Cookies \& Crackers, and Beverages

Product Mix Width
  • Breadth
  • Number of product lines offered
  • Diversity risk generate sales and boost profits, capitalize on their reputation

To attract buyers with different preferences, increase sales and profit, capitalize on economies of scale* companies will increase their length and depth

\The more spent on making, the less it costs to make*

Product line Length
  • Number of product items in a product line
Product line Depth
  • Number of types and sizes offered for each product in the line

9-4 Adjustments to items, lines, and mixes

Product Modifications

  • Quality modification

    • Reduce quality to lower price
    • Increase quality to compete, improve loyalty, raise prices, re-segment the market

  • Functional modification

    • Change in product versatility, effectiveness, convenience, or safety

  • Style modification

    • Aesthetic product change
Planned Obsolescence
  • The process of modifying products so that those products that have already been sold become obsolete before they actually need replacement
  • Very evil thing to do
Repositioning
  • Changing consumer's perceptions
Product line extensions
  • Adding more options to the line
Product line contraction
  • Reducing the number in the line

  • Major benefits include:

    • Resources focused on most important products
    • No longer waste resources trying to improve sales on poorly performing SKUs
    • New products have better chance for success as they will get these resources (that would otherwise be spent on poorly performing products)

9-5 Branding

Originally: Branding meant to mark cattle or other livestock with a hot iron to designate it as one's own. Nowadays, branding has a similar meaning, but instead means to mark a product to designate it as a company's product.

  • Brand

    • Name
    • Term
    • Symbol
    • Design
    • Combination

What a brand stands for includes the feelings, thoughts, and actions associated with the brand

Name, Mark

Brand name: The part of a brand that can be spoken, such as letters, words, and numbers

Brand mark: The elements of a brand that cannot be spoken, such as a logo

Benefits of Branding

The three purposes of branding are...

Product Identification
  • Distinguishes a product from competition
  • Brand equity -- value of company or brand name. High positive reaction to brand = high brand equity
  • Global brand -- a brand available in many different countries. Has to ensure that markets are known, and that the brand makes sense in the new market, and determine where decisions are made (locally or centrally)
Repeat Sales
  • Identifies brands the consumer wants to buy again
  • Brand loyalty
New-product Sales
  • Well-known brands, highly recognized brand names make new-product introductions easier
Branding Strategies

Generic vs. Branded products

  • No frills, no brand name, low-cost product defined by the category
  • Main appeal is low price
  • 30-40% less costly than manufacturer brands

Manufacturer's Brands vs. Private Brands

  • Account for 1 in 5 products sold in Canada
  • Low-cost alternative to manufacturer brand

See Exhibit 9.3: Comparing Manufacturer's and Private Brands from the reseller's perspective

Individual Brands vs. Family Brands

  • Used when products vary in terms of performance

Cobranding

  • Two or more brand names on a products

  • Types:

    • Ingredient -- A branded product is used inside another branded product (e.g. Laptop with "intel inside")
    • Cooperative -- Two brands receive equal treatment (e.g. Hotel chain and credit card company)
    • Complementary -- Enhances a company's presence where it has little or no market share (e.g. Starbucks and Tevana Tea)
Trademarks
  • The exclusive right to use a brand or part of brand

  • Service marks -- trademarks for services

    • Shapes: Coca-cola bottle
    • Ornamental colour or design: Levi's tag
    • Catchy phrase: "Just do it" (Nike)
    • Abbreviations: FedEx (Federal Express)
    • Sounds: THX intro
  • Trademark rights come from use, rather than registration - once a trademark is registered, it must have genuine intention to use it within 3 year of the application being granted
Packaging
  • Containing and protecting

  • Promoting

    • Brand identity, list of ingredients, features, nutritional, directions

  • Facilitating storage, use, convenience

    • Airplane food; single serve foods

  • Facilitating recycling

    • Circular economy movement
Labelling
Persuasive labelling
  • Focuses on a promotional theme or logo and consumer information is secondary
  • Consumers discount claims such as : new, improved, or super
Informational labelling
  • Designed to help the consumer make proper product selections and to lower cognitive dissonance after purchase
  • English and French
  • Consumer Packaging and Labelling Act

9-7 Product Warranties

Warranty protects the buyer and provides essential information about the product

  • Express warranty: simple to extensive

    • Ex. 100% cotton to technical language docs

  • Implied warranty

    • Unwritten guarantee that the good is fit for the purpose for which it is sold
    • Actual warranties vary depending on province or territory
    • Federal protection: Competition Act
